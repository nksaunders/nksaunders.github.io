About Nicholas

Undergraduate at UW in Seattle, earning a B.S. in Astronomy and Comprehensive Physics, and a B.A. in Comparative Literature (Cinema Studies).

My primary research interest is detection and characterization of exoplanets, particularly using the Kepler Space Telescope and subsequent K2 mission.

I love talking about space, particularly exoplanets. There is nothing more exciting than passing on curiousity to someone new. You can find me in the UW Planetarium every week giving shows to the public, or at various events around Seattle like Astronomy on Tap at Peddler Brewing Company or League of Astronomers at UW.

Stellar Simulations

As the Kepler Space Telescope runs out of fuel, its motion will increase in magnitude and become less predictable. I created stellar simulations to understand how increased motion affects noise in K2 light curves.

Everest

I contributed to the development of the Epic Variability Extraction and Removal for Exoplanet Science Targets (Everest) pipeline. Everest was developed by Luger et al. to remove systematic noise from K2 light curves. Using a method called Pixel Level Decorrelation (PLD) based on the work of Deming et al. (2015), we achieved Kepler-like precision in de-trended light curves down to 15th magnitude.

Aperture Fitting

While working with Dr. Luger on the Everest Pipeline, I have developed specific methods to remove instrumental noise from K2 targets, particularly those with bright neighbors or high motion relative to the CCD.

An important task in de-trending K2 light curves is maximizing flux from the target star. To do so, I am developing methods to identify ideal apertures around exoplanet targets.
